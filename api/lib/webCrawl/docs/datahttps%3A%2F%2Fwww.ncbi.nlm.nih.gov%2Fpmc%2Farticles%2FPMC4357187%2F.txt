Participants were 106 children and adolescents with ADHD.</p><p id="__p3" class="p p-last">One group received methylphenidate plus dietary recommendations, while the other group only received methylphenidate. ADHD DSM-IV checklist was used to assess inattentiveness and hyperactivity/impulsivity scores at baseline and at the end of the trial.</p></div><div id="__sec3" class="sec"><h3>Results</h3><p id="__p4" class="p p-first-last">The results revealed no significant difference between the two groups regarding mean age, gender ratio, body mass index, baseline inattentiveness score, and baseline hyperactivity score. Linear regression analysis considering the covariant variables showed that the inattentive score at the end of the trial was significantly associated with the mean change of favorite diet scores.</p></div><div id="__sec4" class="sec"><h3>Conclusion</h3><p id="__p5" class="p p-first-last">This is the first clinical trial examining the effect of overall dietary characteristics rather than a single nutrient on the children formally diagnosed with ADHD. According to the results, un-favorite diet had no effects on inattentive or hyperactivity/impulsivity score. Encouraging the children with ADHD to increase their intake of recommended diet markedly improves their attention.</p></div><div id="__sec5" class="sec sec-last"><h3>Trial registration</h3><p id="__p6" class="p p-first-last">The trial was registered at the Iranian Clinical Trials Registry (Irct ID: <a href="http://www.irct.ir/fa/searchresult.php?keyword=&#x00026;id=3930&#x00026;field=&#x00026;number=29&#x00026;prt=&#x00026;total=&#x00026;m=" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=4357187&amp;issue-id=248478&amp;journal-id=312&amp;FROM=Article%7CFront%20Matter&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="pmc_ext">IRCT201311303930N29</a>).</p></div></div><div class="sec"><strong class="kwd-title">Keywords: </strong><span class="kwd-text">ADHD, Attention, Diet, Clinical trial, Sugar, Cacao, Artificial food color</span></div></div><div id="Sec1" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="Sec1title">Introduction</h2><p id="__p7" class="p p-first">Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) is a common psychiatric disorder in children and adolescents. The neurobiology of ADHD is not clearly known. However, claims have been made that diet is associated with ADHD and infantile malnutrition causes long-lasting attention problems [<a href="#CR1" rid="CR1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583217">1</a>]. However, there is no scientific evidence showing that diet causes ADHD.</p><p id="__p8">There are also some reports about the association of junk food [<a href="#CR2" rid="CR2" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583214">2</a>], &#x0201c;Western&#x0201d; dietary pattern [<a href="#CR3" rid="CR3" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583199">3</a>], and fast food [<a href="#CR4" rid="CR4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583227">4</a>] with ADHD symptoms.</p><p id="__p9">Sweets and sugar are also believed to be associated with ADHD symptoms [<a href="#CR4" rid="CR4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583210">4</a>,<a href="#CR5" rid="CR5" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583233">5</a>]. However, this assumption needs to be examined since some studies have not shown any association between sugar and behavioral problems [<a href="#CR6" rid="CR6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583196">6</a>,<a href="#CR7" rid="CR7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583202">7</a>]. On the contrary, another study reported that less consumption of sugar from fruit snacks increased the risk of ADHD [<a href="#CR8" rid="CR8" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583236">8</a>]. Moreover, none of the studies has examined the effect of sugar on ADHD, but they have assessed its association with behavioral problems and cognition [<a href="#CR6" rid="CR6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583232">6</a>,<a href="#CR7" rid="CR7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583229">7</a>].</p><p id="__p10">A 21-day, double-blind, placebo-controlled, repeated-measure study showed that ingestion of a synthetic food coloring was associated with irritability, restlessness, and sleep disturbance [<a href="#CR9" rid="CR9" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583206">9</a>]. Toxicological, &#x0201c;anti-nutritional,&#x0201d; and hypersensitivity are the three supposed possible mechanisms for the effect of artificial food colors on the children&#x02019;s behaviors [<a href="#CR10" rid="CR10" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583235">10</a>]. However, evidence-based information is needed to support the potential efficacy of restricted diet on ADHD symptoms [<a href="#CR11" rid="CR11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583231">11</a>,<a href="#CR12" rid="CR12" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583211">12</a>]. In a randomized controlled clinical trial, artificial food colorings and a preservative did not show any effects on the children&#x02019;s behavior [<a href="#CR13" rid="CR13" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583218">13</a>]. On the other hand, a systematic review showed that free fatty acid supplementation slightly reduced the ADHD symptoms and that exclusion of artificial food colors might reduce the symptoms in those with food sensitivities [<a href="#CR14" rid="CR14" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583201">14</a>].</p><p id="__p11">One other study reported that food supplements might be as effective as methylphenidate in improvement of some ADHD symptoms [<a href="#CR15" rid="CR15" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583219">15</a>]. Nonetheless, more evidence regarding the efficacy of restricted diets is needed [<a href="#CR14" rid="CR14" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583221">14</a>]. Although omitting some items is supposed to be helpful, their efficacy has not been assessed in controlled clinical trials [<a href="#CR16" rid="CR16" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583204">16</a>]. In fact, it is a neglected research area [<a href="#CR17" rid="CR17" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583198">17</a>]. Moreover, current literature suffers from publication bias and small non-generalizable samples [<a href="#CR18" rid="CR18" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583223">18</a>]. Thus, further investigations are recommended to be conducted on the effect of diet on ADHD [<a href="#CR18" rid="CR18" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583230">18</a>].</p><p id="__p12">One study showed that intake of sweetened desserts, fried food, and salt are associated with more attention and behavioral problems while a balanced diet, regular meals, and a high intake of dairy products and vegetables are associated with less attention and behavioral problems [<a href="#CR19" rid="CR19" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583234">19</a>]. However, the cross-sectional design of that study did not allow the authors to investigate a cause and effect relationship [<a href="#CR19" rid="CR19" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583222">19</a>].</p><p id="__p13" class="p p-last">Moreover, as mentioned above, the current literature commonly associated some dietary factors with ADHD, while the overall dietary characteristics have been less taken into account [<a href="#CR4" rid="CR4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583195">4</a>]. Also, no studies have assessed the effect of overall dietary intervention on ADHD. This implies that assessment of the effect of overall diet rather than a single nutrient could be more informative [<a href="#CR4" rid="CR4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583212">4</a>]. Therefore, we designed a trial to examine the effect of these recommended variables on the children with ADHD. This is the first randomized controlled clinical trial investigating the adjuvant effect of &#x0201c;un-favored food diet&#x0201d; and &#x0201c;favored food diet&#x0201d; on treatment of the children and adolescents diagnosed with ADHD.</p></div><div id="Sec2" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="Sec2title">Methods</h2><p id="__p14" class="p p-first">The present trial was conducted on a group of children diagnosed with ADHD. The diagnosis was made through face-to-face interviews with the children and their parents and considering their teachers&#x02019; reports. The diagnosis was made according to DSM-IV diagnostic criteria supported by KSADS [<a href="#CR20" rid="CR20" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583207">20</a>].</p><p id="__p15">Assessments for both groups were performed at baseline and 1 month after the onset of the trial.</p><p id="__p16">The patients were from both genders aged 5 to 14 years old with ADHD. Those with a serious medical condition or lack willingness to enter this trial were not entered. The patients/caregivers need to provide written informed consent.</p><p id="__p17" class="p">ADHD severity was measured using ADHD checklist [<a href="#CR21" rid="CR21" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583197">21</a>]. This checklist consists of 18 items, including 9 items for inattentiveness and 9 items for hyperactivity/impulsivity. The items are in fact the symptoms of ADHD according to DSM-IV. The instrument is scored through a Likert-type scale. The scores of inattentiveness and hyperactivity/impulsivity range from 0 to 27 with higher scores representing worse conditions.</p><div id="Sec3" class="sec"><h3>Anthropometric measurements</h3><p id="__p18" class="p p-first-last">The children&#x02019;s weight, height, and body mass index were measured.</p></div><div id="Sec4" class="sec"><h3>Dietary intake</h3><p id="__p19" class="p p-first">Dietary intake was reported by the parents using a food frequency questionnaire for the last 1 month [<a href="#CR4" rid="CR4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583228">4</a>]. It was asked based on the nutrient similarity of foods. The food groups and their items were developed by a previous study on an Iranian sample [<a href="#CR4" rid="CR4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583225">4</a>]. According to that study, some examples of healthy foods are fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and dairy. On the other hand, some un-favored foods include sugar, soft drinks, commercially produced fruit juices, and sauces. Also, one of the items asked about the regular three meals a day intake. The parents in both groups reported the frequency of foods intake through a Likert-type scale ranging from never (0) to almost always (4).</p><p id="__p20" class="p p-last">We provided two lists of foods the first of which including the foods which were recommended to be eaten [<a href="#CR4" rid="CR4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583224">4</a>]. Some examples of these foods were diary, homemade fruit juices, vegetables, and low-fat meat. The other list included the foods which were recommended to be eaten as less as possible [<a href="#CR4" rid="CR4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583205">4</a>]. Some examples of this list were sugar, soft drinks, commercially produced fruit juices, and sauces. We called the first list as the &#x0201c;Favored foods&#x0201d; and the second one as &#x0201c;Un-favored foods.&#x0201d; In addition, the list requested the parents to provide their children with three regular meals.</p></div><div id="Sec5" class="sec"><h3>Groups</h3><p id="__p21" class="p p-first">In this study, the patients were randomly allocated into two groups using a random number generator. Both groups received methylphenidate. However, one group was provided the list of favored and un-favored foods. Besides, one researcher explained the items of these lists to the parents. The other group, however, only received the medication.</p><p id="__p22" class="p p-last">The study was approved by the Ethics Committee of Shiraz University of Medical Sciences. In addition, written informed consents for taking part in the study were obtained from the patients or their parents. This trial was performed in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki 1975.</p></div><div id="Sec6" class="sec sec-last"><h3>Statistical analysis</h3><p id="__p23" class="p p-first">All the statistical analyses were performed using the SPSS statistical software. The categorical variables, such as gender ratio, were compared between the two groups using chi-squared test. In addition, <em>t</em>-test was used to compare the continuous variables, including age, body mass index, mean score of inattentiveness, and mean score of hyperactivity/impulsivity, between the two groups. Besides, the two groups were compared regarding the mean differences of favored food and un-favored food scores using <em>t</em>-test. The mean differences of inattentiveness and hyperactivity/impulsivity scores were also compared through <em>t</em>-test.</p><p id="__p24" class="p p-last">In the second round of the statistical analyses, two separate linear regression analyses were performed using Enter method. The degrees of changes in favorite and un-favorite foods were considered as continuous variables. Moreover, group, gender, body mass index, dose of methylphenidate intake at the beginning of the trial, dose of methylphenidate intake during the trial, mean difference of favored food score, mean difference of un-favored food score, mean score of inattentiveness at baseline, and mean score of hyperactivity/impulsivity at baseline were considered as the independent variables (covariates). On the other hand, mean scores of inattentiveness and hyperactivity/impulsivity at the end of the trial were considered as dependent variables. <em>P</em> value less than 0.05 was considered as statistically significant.</p></div></div><div id="Sec7" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="Sec7title">Results</h2><p id="__p25" class="p p-first">Among the 110 patients, 106 ones (53 in each group) agreed to participate in the study. Performing the analyses considering the groups as categorical variables did not show any difference between the outcomes of the two groups. The degree of cooperation between the parents and children for applying the dietary recommendations is a continuous variable. Moreover, the patients might have performed the intervention differently. Some of them might have dramatically increased the favorite food intake, while some others might have not. Therefore, we performed the statistical analyses considering the dietary intervention as a continuous variable using the food questionnaire. Therefore, we included the degree of compliance to our recommendations as a covariate factor. In this analysis, the mean changes of favorite and un-favorite diet scores were calculated. This change of scores was considered as an independent variable in linear regression analysis. The most striking finding of this study was the negative correlation between the increase of favorite diet scores and the scores of inattentiveness severity at the end of the trial. However, the results of regression analysis showed that none of the other variables, i.e., age, gender, medication dosage, body mass index, and mean change of un-favorite foods score, was significantly associated with the inattentiveness scores. This implies that the children who markedly increased their intake of favorite foods experienced lesser inattentiveness in comparison to those with none or less increase of favorite diet intake. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first study which examined the effect of both favorite and un-favorite diets on the children with ADHD. We could not find any similar trials to compare our results.</p><p id="__p41">The present study showed that un-favorite diet had no effects on inattentive or hyperactivity/impulsivity scores. Previous reports about the association between junk food [<a href="#CR2" rid="CR2" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583203">2</a>] and &#x0201c;Western&#x0201d; dietary pattern [<a href="#CR3" rid="CR3" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583208">3</a>] and ADHD were descriptive studies rather than interventional clinical trials. In addition, the findings regarding the relationship between sugar and ADHD symptoms are very controversial and many studies have not shown this relation [<a href="#CR6" rid="CR6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583213">6</a>,<a href="#CR7" rid="CR7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583209">7</a>]. Meanwhile, one study showed that less consumption of sugar from fruit snacks increased the risk of ADHD [<a href="#CR8" rid="CR8" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583226">8</a>]. However, long-term trials are needed to examine the effect of un-favorite diet on inattention.</p><p id="__p42">Overall, our results are in agreement with those of a descriptive study indicating that a balanced diet, regular meals, and a high intake of dairy products and vegetables were associated with less attention and behavioral problems [<a href="#CR19" rid="CR19" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583216">19</a>].</p><p id="__p43">Nevertheless, descriptive studies conducted on the effect of synthetic food colorings on attention were not conclusive [<a href="#CR9" rid="CR9" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583200">9</a>]. For instance, one study was not a clinical trial and also evaluated the association between the food colorings and some behaviors rather than ADHD [<a href="#CR9" rid="CR9" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583215">9</a>]. One other study also suggested that omitting some food items could be helpful; however, its efficacy has never been examined in controlled clinical trials [<a href="#CR16" rid="CR16" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_434583220">16</a>].</p><p id="__p44">This study had some limitations. First of all, the sample size was relatively small. The duration of the intervention was also relatively short. However, we were not sure if we could get the Ethic Committee&#x02019;s approval due to the lack of any strong evidence about the usefulness and lack of negative consequences of the recommended diet on the children with ADHD. Moreover, although some of the covariant variables were taken into account by statistical adjustment, the patients were taking the medications concurrently. Therefore, they were stable of the medication. So, the dietary intervention was an adjuvant.</p><p id="__p45">Another explanation is that children with better attention at baseline more successfully followed our recommendations. However, we considered this as a covariate factor in the regression analysis. Therefore, it does not seem that the effect of intervention of the attention score be just due to baseline attention score.</p><p id="__p46">We also could not consider a control group including the patients with dietary intervention alone. In fact, there was no evidence that dietary intervention alone would be effective, and we probably were not able to convince the Ethics Committee to omit the medication in one group. Furthermore, sampling and the trial were performed in one season and included only a clinical sample from a university-affiliated child psychiatry clinic. Therefore, generalization of the results to other seasons or other clinics should be performed with caution. Further studies may also include objective biochemical measurements.</p><p id="__p47" class="p p-last">Nevertheless, this is the first randomized controlled clinical trial which examined the effect of overall dietary characteristics rather than a single nutrient on the children formally diagnosed with ADHD.</p></div><div id="Sec9" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="Sec9title">Conclusion</h2><p id="__p48" class="p p-first-last">Dietary recommendations by the investigators led to a significant improvement in diet compared to no intervention. However, the two experimental groups did not show different amounts of improvement in measures of attention. Nevertheless, when both groups were combined, there was a significant relationship between improvements in favorite dietary behavior and improvements in measures of attention. Un-favorite diet intake was not associated with improvements in either inattentiveness or hyperactivity/impulsivity.</p></div><div id="__ackidm140080870721696" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__ackidm140080870721696title">Acknowledgements</h2><div class="sec"><p id="__p53">The authors are grateful for Ms. A.
